# FABLE-ONE-WINDOW-RUNBOOK — Strategic Opportunity Decision Document

Date: 2026-07-06. Produced by the "MenuVoice Strategic Opportunity Analysis" run (the trigger scheduled for 2026-07-05 that was auto-disabled; executed manually the next day). Companion live log: FABLE-PROGRESS.md.

**The decision in one sentence:** MenuVoice should build a restaurant-facing product — a hosted, verified, accessible menu page that restaurants pay for — because it is the only initiative that simultaneously solves the app's three existential problems: no users, no revenue, and menu data nobody can fully trust.

---

## 1. What MenuVoice is today, and where it is weak

**What it is.** A one-month-old, ~9,300-line React + Vite web app with 17 Vercel serverless functions. A blind or low-vision diner can capture a menu three ways (guided camera scan with audio coaching, restaurant-name web search, direct URL/PDF), the server parses it with OpenAI into a structured menu, and the user either browses it with VoiceOver (carefully engineered heading rotor) or holds a voice conversation about it. The engineering depth is unusual for the project's age: a provenance model (official vs third-party source, location scope, freshness, completeness), explicit-vs-inferred allergen classification with disciplined "the restaurant lists X" vs "may contain X, not confirmed" wording, strict turn-taking, barge-in, earcons, a full telemetry pipeline into Postgres with a live dashboard and a daily email digest.

**Where it is weak or underdeveloped:**

1. **Zero real users.** The analytics apparatus is elaborate, but PROGRESS.md records the events table as "almost empty," the morning report's core question is "did anyone use MenuVoice," and the internal-exclusion list is the founder's own two emails. Every workstream doc ends in "needs a real device / real user test" that has not happened. The product has never been validated by a single blind diner at a real table.
2. **No revenue surface at all.** No pricing, no restaurant-facing anything, no partner story — while every session burns OpenAI vision + chat + Whisper + TTS tokens. Costs scale linearly with the usage the product doesn't yet have.
3. **Menu data cannot be fully trusted — by the app's own admission.** The recurring theme across REVIEW.md, PROGRESS.md, and FIXES-NEEDED.md is data reliability: the Browserless fallback is dead so JS-heavy chain sites fail, scanner thresholds are "tuned on theory," incomplete-menu detection is heuristic, and every allergen answer must hedge because ingredients are LLM-inferred. The honesty layer (a genuine strength) is compensating for data the app cannot verify.
4. **The consumer moat is eroding from below.** Be My AI and ChatGPT voice already photograph menus and discuss them, free. "AI reads the menu aloud" is being commoditized by general-purpose assistants with billion-dollar budgets. MenuVoice's craft (turn-taking, rotor, provenance) is real but is UX polish on top of the same unverified data those free tools use.

## 2. The highest-leverage opportunities identified

**O1 — Restaurant-facing verified accessible menu ("MenuVoice for Restaurants").** A hosted, WCAG-conformant HTML menu page per restaurant (e.g. `menuvoice.app/m/luigis-montclair`), generated by the existing parsing engine from the restaurant's own menu, *confirmed by the restaurant* (items, prices, declared allergens), linked from the restaurant's website and a table QR code. Restaurants pay a small monthly fee. Verified restaurants appear with a trusted badge and first-class treatment in the consumer app.

**O2 — Consumer verified-menu network.** Keep the consumer app the sole focus; invest in menu caching, cross-user shared menus, freshness re-checking, and user feedback ("this menu is wrong") to build a proprietary corpus of community-verified menus.

**O3 — Enterprise chain accessibility deals.** Go straight to US chains (the Nando's/PizzaExpress/Wetherspoon equivalents — Panera, Chipotle, Cheesecake Factory), selling accessible-menu coverage across hundreds of locations per contract.

**O4 — Distribution blitz into the blind community.** Make acquisition the product: NFB chapters and conventions, Lighthouse organizations, AppleVis, O&M instructors — push for the first hundred real blind users of the existing app before building anything new.

**O5 — Platform integration layer.** Become the accessibility layer for restaurant platforms (Toast, Square, Popmenu, BentoBox, DoorDash): they expose menu data, MenuVoice renders the accessible/voice experience, distribution rides their sales channel.

## 3. Why each could create meaningful business value

- **O1** attacks a documented, urgent, financially painful buyer problem. In 2025, restaurants were the single most-sued industry for digital accessibility — 1,368 ADA lawsuits, 34.7% of all filings — and inaccessible menu PDFs are cited as the easiest violation for plaintiffs to find. The standard prescribed fix is exactly what MenuVoice's engine already produces: a structured, screen-reader-accessible HTML menu. The category's business model is proven in the UK by Good Food Talks (restaurants pay a low monthly fee; 2,150+ venues; national chains as customers; acquired by Nutritics), and the US equivalent is effectively vacant — Menus4All is a nonprofit aggregator, and BentoBox/Popmenu sell whole-website compliance, not a blind-diner destination or an at-table voice experience. ~675,000 US restaurant locations; independents already pay $50–350/month for digital tools. Even 1,000 locations at $29–49/month is $350–600K ARR with tiny marginal cost, and every paying restaurant also hands MenuVoice the thing it cannot get any other way: verified menu and allergen data.
- **O2** builds real user value (fresher, more reliable menus) and a data asset. But community verification needs a community — with zero current users it compounds nothing — and it produces no revenue while increasing OpenAI spend. It also does not upgrade allergen answers past "may contain," because users cannot confirm what a kitchen puts in a dish.
- **O3** is where the real money eventually is (one chain deal = hundreds of locations, and chains are the primary ADA lawsuit targets). But enterprise procurement demands security review, references, an SLA, and a track record a one-month-old solo project does not have. Attempting it now means 9–18 months of sales cycle with nothing shipping.
- **O4** addresses the most alarming single fact (zero users) and costs almost nothing. But it is a campaign, not a product initiative, and it drives users into an app whose data problem is unsolved and whose economics are negative. Alone, it accelerates burn and exposes the trust gap sooner.
- **O5** offers massive theoretical distribution but makes MenuVoice's roadmap hostage to platform partnerships a solo unknown founder is unlikely to land; BentoBox has already shown platforms prefer to build accessibility features in-house.

## 4. Why O1 beats the others right now

Scored against the criteria in the brief:

- **Value to blind and low-vision users:** highest of the five. A verified menu is the only path from "may contain peanuts, but the restaurant does not confirm it" to "the restaurant lists peanuts in this dish." For the one safety-critical feature — allergens — verification is not polish, it is the product. It also fixes reliability at the moments that matter (no scraping failure, no dead-Browserless 404, no half-parsed PDF) for exactly the restaurants users care most about.
- **Likelihood the customer pays:** the only option with a proven paying buyer *and* an active forcing function (lawsuit wave, +24% YoY). The pitch is one sentence: "Your menu PDF is the #1 thing accessibility plaintiffs look for; for $29/month you get an accessible menu page, a QR code, and inclusion in an app blind diners actually use."
- **Strategic defensibility:** free general-purpose AI can read any menu, but it can never be *restaurant-confirmed*. Each verified restaurant is a node in a two-sided network (more verified menus → more blind users default to MenuVoice → "diners find you here" becomes part of the restaurant pitch) that neither ChatGPT nor an overlay vendor can replicate without doing the same restaurant-by-restaurant work.
- **Speed to validation:** weeks, not quarters. The parsing engine, provenance model, allergen wording, and voice conversation all exist; the missing pieces are a public read-only page, a slug, and a founder-assisted intake flow. Ten local-restaurant conversations will validate willingness to pay faster than any amount of consumer growth hacking.
- **Technical feasibility:** the hosted menu page is the *easiest* major feature the codebase could ship — server-rendered HTML from an existing `ParsedMenu` + `MenuProvenance`, no camera, no scraping, no live LLM required for browse mode.
- **Long-term position:** O1 is the on-ramp to O3 (chains buy after independents prove it), makes O4 worth doing (drive users to an app with verified data), subsumes O2's goal (verification restaurants pay to provide beats verification users donate), and gives O5 something to integrate later. The reverse is not true for any other option.

Why not do O4 (get users) first? Because the two must ship together and O1 is the constraint. Real blind users are required to validate O1 — "blind diners actually use this" is half the restaurant pitch — so the initiative below explicitly includes recruiting them. But sending a hundred users into the current app without verified data invites them to churn on the first mis-scraped menu or hedged allergen answer, and first impressions in a tight-knit community (NFB chapters, AppleVis) are nearly impossible to reset.

## 5. The initiative: MenuVoice Verified — accessible menus restaurants stand behind

Build the restaurant-facing layer:

1. **Public accessible menu page** — `/m/{slug}`: a hosted, fast, screen-reader-first HTML rendering of a verified `ParsedMenu`, using the heading-rotor structure, AAA contrast, and allergen wording already engineered in the app. Works with zero install for any diner who follows the link or QR code; offers "talk to this menu" (the existing voice conversation) as progressive enhancement.
2. **Founder-assisted restaurant intake** — restaurant sends a menu (PDF/URL/photos); the existing engine parses it; the restaurant reviews a proof and confirms items, prices, and declared allergens; a human-in-the-loop confirmation flag upgrades provenance to a new `verified` source type.
3. **Verified integration in the consumer app** — find-by-name checks verified restaurants first (no scraping, no web search, instant and exact); verified menus carry a spoken trust line ("This menu was provided and confirmed by the restaurant on July 2nd"); allergen answers for restaurant-declared data drop the hedge.
4. **The commercial wrapper** — a one-page pitch on the marketing site, a QR table-tent PDF generator, an "Accessible menu" link snippet for the restaurant's own website, and a founding-partner offer (first 10–20 restaurants free-for-life or deeply discounted in exchange for feedback and a case study; $29–49/month/location thereafter).

## 6. What success looks like

**For users:** a blind diner follows a QR code or opens the app at a verified restaurant and gets the exact current menu instantly — no scan, no search roulette — with allergen answers that say "the restaurant lists shellfish in this dish" instead of a hedge. Ordering happens with no sighted help and no uncertainty about whether the menu is real, current, or complete.

**For the business, within ~90 days of shipping:** 10+ verified restaurants live (any mix of paid and founding-partner); at least 3 with money or a signed LOI attached; 25+ real blind or low-vision users recruited through community channels who have used a verified menu at least once; at least one restaurant reporting a blind diner used it at the table; verified-menu sessions measurably outperforming scraped-menu sessions on completion (telemetry already distinguishes source). Those numbers prove the loop; scale is then a sales problem, not a product bet.

## 7. How this leads to revenue, growth, trust, retention, defensibility

- **Revenue:** the first dollars MenuVoice can plausibly earn, from a buyer with a legal forcing function, on a subscription that costs almost nothing to serve (a verified menu page needs no LLM call to render). Path: independents at $29–49/month → local groups → chains (O3) at negotiated per-location pricing.
- **Growth:** every verified restaurant recruits users for the app (QR on the table, link on their site) — restaurants become the acquisition channel, which no amount of consumer marketing budget could buy. Accessibility organizations get a concrete, non-charity reason to promote it ("these restaurants committed to you").
- **Trust:** verification converts the app's biggest weakness (hedged, inferred data) into its headline strength, on the exact axis — allergens — where trust is safety.
- **Retention:** a diner returns to the app because their neighborhood's verified restaurants live there; a restaurant retains because canceling means removing an accessibility accommodation it may have cited publicly (and to plaintiffs' lawyers) — churn resistance most SaaS never gets.
- **Defensibility:** a two-sided network of restaurant-confirmed menus, built restaurant by restaurant. Free AI can describe a photo; it cannot get Luigi's to confirm the carbonara has guanciale this month. If Good Food Talks/Nutritics enters the US later, incumbency in US blind-community distribution and a verified base is the defense — and the Nutritics acquisition itself validates the exit path.

## 8. Biggest risks, assumptions, and what must be validated

1. **Willingness to pay (the assumption everything rests on).** UK precedent and lawsuit pressure suggest yes; nobody has asked a US independent restaurant for this specific product at this price. *Validate first, before building anything*: 10 in-person/call conversations with local restaurants; target 3 yeses to a free founding pilot and honest price feedback.
2. **Legal positioning.** MenuVoice must sell "an accessible menu experience your blind customers actually use," never "ADA compliance" — the FTC fined an overlay vendor $1M in 2025 for compliance guarantees, and 22.6% of 2025 suits hit sites *with* accessibility widgets. The differentiation (a real accessible experience, not an overlay) is the marketing message, and the word "compliance" stays out of contracts.
3. **Allergen liability.** Restaurant-declared data reduces moral risk but adds legal surface. Mitigation is already half-built (explicit vs inferred classification): verified wording must still say "the restaurant lists…" (attribution, not guarantee), keep the cross-contamination caveat, and terms of service must put menu accuracy on the restaurant. This needs a real lawyer before the first paid contract.
4. **Two-sided cold start.** Mitigated structurally: the app keeps working for *every* restaurant via the existing scan/search paths — verified is a premium layer, not a gate — and the sell to restaurant #1 works with zero users because the accessible page + QR has standalone value.
5. **Solo-founder sales capacity.** The model is founder-led sales in one metro (the docs suggest northern New Jersey) until the pitch converts repeatably. If 10 conversations produce zero interest at any price, that is a cheap, decisive kill signal — stop and reassess toward O4+O2.
6. **Menu update burden.** Verified menus go stale; a restaurant that never re-confirms erodes the badge. First version: freshness date spoken aloud, email nudge after 60 days, "confirmed X ago" honesty. Do not build automated change detection yet.
7. **Real blind users still unrecruited.** The 90-day success criteria force this; without the user half, the restaurant half is an accessibility widget by another name.

## 9. Lean execution approach

**Validate before building (week 1–2, no code):** the 10 restaurant conversations; 5 conversations with blind users or advocacy contacts (NFB NJ, Lighthouse) about whether verified menus + QR would change where they eat. Kill/continue decision on evidence.

**First version must include (build weeks, in order):**
1. `/m/{slug}` public menu page: server-rendered accessible HTML from a stored verified menu (KV/Postgres), rotor-correct headings, AAA contrast, freshness + verification line, allergen display using the existing explicit/inferred wording, works perfectly with VoiceOver in Safari with no JS required for browse.
2. `verified` provenance source type end-to-end: storage → find-by-name short-circuit → spoken trust line → allergen wording upgrade for restaurant-declared allergens only.
3. Founder-side intake: a script/minimal admin path that takes a menu PDF/URL, runs the existing parser, produces a human-reviewable proof (simple HTML), and publishes on approval. A Google Form + email is an acceptable restaurant-facing front end at first.
4. QR + link kit: printable table-tent PDF and a copy-paste "Accessible menu" HTML snippet.
5. One marketing-site page with the founding-partner offer.

**Can wait:** self-serve restaurant dashboard and payments (invoice or Stripe payment link manually), multi-location management, automated freshness re-checks, menu change detection, analytics for restaurants ("N diners viewed your menu" — add once telemetry shows real traffic), Spanish/other languages, chain outreach.

**Explicitly do not build:** anything overlay/widget-shaped; automated WCAG scanning of restaurant websites (different business, crowded); ordering/payments/POS integration; native iOS/Android apps; a self-serve AI onboarding flow before ten restaurants have been onboarded by hand; any claim, page, or contract word promising legal compliance.

## 10. Implementation prompt for the next Fable run

> You are implementing **MenuVoice Verified** in the menuvoice2 repository — the restaurant-facing verified accessible menu layer chosen in FABLE-ONE-WINDOW-RUNBOOK.md (read it and FABLE-PROGRESS.md first; read CLAUDE.md, PRODUCT.md, and AGENTS.md for conventions).
>
> **Business goal:** let a restaurant supply and confirm its menu so MenuVoice can (a) host it as a public, screen-reader-first accessible menu page restaurants will pay for, and (b) serve it in the consumer app as instant, trusted, restaurant-confirmed data — upgrading allergen answers from "may contain (inferred)" to "the restaurant lists…".
>
> **User problem:** blind and low-vision diners cannot trust scraped or photographed menus — freshness, completeness, and especially allergens are all inferred. Restaurants face ADA lawsuit pressure over inaccessible menu PDFs and need a real accessible menu, not an overlay.
>
> **Scope — build these, in this order:**
> 1. **Data model:** add a `verified` source type to `MenuProvenance` (`src/types.ts`, `api/_menuCore.ts` classification, `src/lib/provenance.ts` speech). A verified menu record = `ParsedMenu` + provenance + slug + restaurant display info (name, address, phone) + `verifiedAt` timestamp + per-item restaurant-declared allergen lists. Store in Vercel KV (or Postgres if KV shape fights you) under the slug.
> 2. **Public page:** `api/menu-page.ts` (or a rewrite route in `vercel.json`) serving `/m/{slug}` as server-rendered HTML. Requirements: works fully without JavaScript; heading structure identical to the app's MenuDocument rotor design (h1 restaurant → h2 category with item count → h3 dish as a single rotor stop folding name/price/description; details aria-hidden inside the h3 label, matching `ConversationScreen.tsx`); WCAG AAA contrast using the app's existing dark theme tokens; a verification line ("Menu provided and confirmed by {name} on {date}") near the top; restaurant-declared allergens rendered per item as "Contains: … (listed by the restaurant)"; inferred allergens keep the existing "may contain … not confirmed" wording — never mix the two up; a link into the app to talk to this menu by voice. No emojis anywhere in text that will be spoken. Page must be fast and cost ~zero to serve (no LLM call on render).
> 3. **Consumer-app integration:** in find-by-name (`api/find-menu.ts`), check the verified store first (normalized name + location match) and return the verified menu with its provenance before any web search; in the conversation opening line and provenance voice answers, speak the verified trust line; in allergen answers (`src/lib/openai.ts` system prompt + `src/lib/provenance.ts`), allow unhedged "the restaurant lists X" ONLY for restaurant-declared allergens on verified menus, keep every existing caution otherwise, and always keep the cross-contamination caveat.
> 4. **Intake tooling (founder-side, not public):** `scripts/verify-restaurant.mjs` that takes `--slug --name --address --menu <pdf|url|image paths>`, runs the existing parse pipeline, writes a human-reviewable HTML proof to `scratch/proofs/{slug}.html`, and on `--publish` writes the record to the store. Restaurant-declared allergens are entered via a simple JSON/CSV sidecar the founder fills in from the restaurant's confirmation — do not let the LLM fabricate "declared" allergens under any circumstances.
> 5. **QR/link kit:** extend the script with `--kit` to emit a printable table-tent HTML/PDF ("Accessible menu — scan or visit menuvoice.app/m/{slug}") and a copy-paste website snippet.
> 6. **Marketing page:** one page on the existing marketing site (`website/`) pitching the founding-partner offer. Follow CLAUDE.md's writing rules: factual, no salesy AI-slop, no fabricated claims, no compliance guarantees — say "accessible menu experience," never "ADA compliant."
>
> **Constraints:** all OpenAI calls stay server-side (server env vars, not VITE_). Do not break any existing flow (camera scan, find-by-name for unverified restaurants, URL/PDF, saved restaurants, sync). Voice-first and VoiceOver compatibility take priority over anything visual. No emojis in speech output. Keep serving costs near zero for the public page.
>
> **Testing expectations:** unit tests (existing `npm test` node:test setup) for the verified store round-trip, verified-first find matching, and allergen wording selection (declared vs inferred — this is safety-critical, test the wording strings exactly); an integration check that `/m/{slug}` renders with no JS and passes the repo's a11y audit (`npm run a11y:audit` pattern, axe zero violations); `npm run build` green. Manually document (in FABLE-PROGRESS.md) the VoiceOver test script for a real device: rotor through the public page, verified find-by-name in the app, and one allergen question on a verified menu.
>
> **Do not build:** restaurant self-serve dashboard, auth, or payments; POS/ordering integrations; automated menu change detection; WCAG scanning of external sites; native apps; anything overlay-like; any compliance-guarantee language. Seed one real local restaurant (with the founder's data) plus the demo menu as fixtures.
>
> Maintain FABLE-PROGRESS.md throughout the run. Ship on a feature branch, build green, and open a draft PR describing what a restaurant and a blind diner can now do that they could not before.
